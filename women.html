Before the War:
- By the 19th century, most countries granted women suffrage.
- Working-class women typically worked in factories or as domestically serving the rich.
- Women's work was thought of as being less important than the men's, despite working with items that were in high demand.
During the War:
- New jobs popped up for women to fill since their husbands were off at war.
- Women began working in formerly male-dominated jobs, like ticket collectors, railway guards, officers, firefighters, clerks, etc.
- Associations such as the Red Cross relied on thousands of female volunteers.
- Women remained being paid less than men.
- Thousands also served in the Army Corps or Navy Corps as nurses.
After the War:
- The men returned to their normal jobs and the women working in those fields declined.
- Women trying to find real work that utilized their skills were slandered by the press as they "took up ex-servicemen's jobs"
- Unequality forced them back into working into standard 'women's work', like domestic work & textiling.

Sources: strikingwomen.org, theworldwar.org, study.com
